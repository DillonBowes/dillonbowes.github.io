The Economics of Wartime Trading (Project Development)

This project examined how conflict environments reshape sectoral performance across energy, defense, and infrastructure-linked firms. The work combined scraped financial data, economic theory, and historical case analysis.

It evolved through multiple iterations, moving from abstract optimization models to company-level analysis incorporating firms such as ExxonMobil and Lockheed Martin, alongside historical parallels like the Russia–Ukraine conflict.

Independent Investment & Black Box Modeling

This work focused on building a data-driven trading model designed to reduce discretionary decision-making in foreign exchange markets.

The system integrates quantitative signals, risk constraints, and automated decision rules implemented in Python to evaluate trade opportunities based on structured inputs rather than intuition.

Operationalizing AI Assurance: Translating Maturity and Risk Frameworks into Measurable Practice

This research develops an operational framework for translating AI governance and risk management standards into measurable organizational assessment tools.

It builds on MITRE AIMM and NIST AI RMF by defining six structured assurance pillars and mapping them to observable evidence for maturity scoring across organizations.

A heatmap-based maturity matrix was developed to visualize governance gaps and enable comparative assessment across systems and organizations.

The work was presented at SPIE in Washington, D.C. and peer-reviewed for publication, confirming external validation of the methodology.

Economies of Scale in Artificial Intelligence Innovation

This paper studies whether AI innovation exhibits economies of scale at the firm level, given the high fixed costs and low marginal deployment costs of AI systems.

Using Compustat firm data and USPTO AI-related patents, the analysis estimates how innovation output scales with firm size using regression models including fixed effects and nonlinear specifications.

Results show consistent positive scaling: larger firms produce more AI patents, with elasticities ranging from 0.15 to 0.23, suggesting sublinear but meaningful economies of scale in AI innovation.
